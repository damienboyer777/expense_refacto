Introduction

Where we are today.

This document doesn't exist to prescribe a method. It exists because a shift is happening around us, and design needs a shared response.

The numbers

A partial snapshot of AI tool adoption at PayFit. It does not include Codex, Cursor, or other platforms the company subscribes to. But it's enough to notice the gap.

5 / 47
designers among Claude Code users at PayFit
9 / 222
designers among PayFiters with a GitHub account
30
engineers using Claude Code, the baseline we're measured against

Engineers have show-and-tells, peering sessions, and trial subscriptions. Designers mostly explore alone. The AI guild is open and transverse, and it isn't a substitute for a design-specific learning system.

What we're noticing

Not hypotheticals. Patterns that are becoming common across teams.

  • Non-designers are producing interface work. PMs show up with wireframes done in Claude Design. Engineers prototype flows directly in code. Sometimes it's great collaboration, sometimes we find out after the fact.
  • The demand for design guidelines is shifting. People ask where to find the UX and content guidelines they can feed into Claude or Cursor. The Design System is not yet structured for AI consumption.
  • Individual effort isn't scaling. Each designer configures access, learns the tool, finds their use cases on their own. What one learns doesn't reliably reach the rest of the team.
The real problem

It isn't "the design team doesn't use AI enough." It's that a new way of building is forming around us, while we explore individually, without a shared framework, without a visible collective position.

What this document proposes

  1. Name what we never delegate, so our craft stays legible to the company.
  2. Map where AI actually helps, and where it promises more than it delivers.
  3. Organize how we level up together, not each on our own.
  4. Open a structured experimentation space, instead of waiting for the right method to appear.

This is a V1. It is written to be criticized, reshaped, and iterated on as a team.

What we never delegate
The framework

What we never delegate.

Before talking about what AI does well, we draw the red lines. Not out of fear of the tool, out of clarity about our craft.

Defining the problem

AI executes an intention. It does not formulate one.

Understanding what a user is actually trying to accomplish, why they're struggling, which moment of their professional life we're touching, that's design thinking. Not interface generation. A designer who lets AI define the problem has delegated the job, not a task.

Decisions that affect vulnerable users

In an HR product, every interface decision has real consequences on real people. A misread payslip, a miscalibrated compliance alert, an onboarding flow that forgets an edge case, those are not bugs. They're impacts.

AI can propose, compare, accelerate. It cannot carry the responsibility of a choice that affects a vulnerable user: a new employee, an HR lead under end-of-month pressure, a director discovering a new legal obligation.

What we choose not to build

Design craft is as much about what we leave out as what we put in.

AI has an addition bias. It adds options, fields, features. The judgment that removes, simplifies, says "this doesn't deserve a screen", refuses a product request, that stays with us.

The voice of the product

Content design is not filler. It's a stance on the relationship we build with users.

AI can generate 50 phrasings. Choosing which one is PayFit, which one respects a user who's struggling, which one doesn't ring false, that's a judgment built on accumulated knowledge of the product and the people.

Signals we watch for

Knowing the red lines isn't enough. Judgment erodes quietly, not suddenly. These are the patterns we try to catch early, in ourselves and in each other, without making it a performance review.

  • Outputs stop surprising anyone. Your work starts looking like everyone else's AI-assisted work. Same rhythms, same phrasing, same safe layouts. That's conformity bias leaking through.
  • Critique gets harder to articulate. When asked "why this choice over that one?", the answer is thinner than it used to be. Explanations become descriptions of what the AI produced, not justifications of a point of view.
  • First drafts get accepted too easily. The iteration muscle atrophies. What was once a starting point becomes the final version because it's good enough.
  • Edge cases vanish from the work. Empty states, error states, unhappy paths, accessibility, localization. If these quietly stop appearing in your specs, AI is driving and not getting challenged.
  • You can't explain the work without the tool. If rebuilding the same piece without AI would feel impossibly hard, you've delegated more than a task.
How we use this

Not as a checklist to judge. As prompts for design review. "Has anything here stopped surprising us?" is a healthier question than "did you use AI?".

The test we use

Heuristic

Can a designer who delegates this to AI still defend their job against a sensitized PM and an engineer who knows how to prompt?

If the answer is no, it's a red line. We don't cross it, even under pressure for speed.

Where AI actually helps
The framework

Where AI actually helps.

The structure follows moments of the design workflow, not roles. A PD, a service designer, and a content designer often face the same moments, just on different artifacts.

Research & framing

Where it helps
  • Synthesizing a corpus of interviews, extracting recurring themes.
  • Reformulating an ambiguous brief, surfacing what's unsaid.
  • Exhaustive competitor scans, where we'd otherwise stop at two captures.
Where it overpromises
  • It smooths out weak signals. The three uncomfortable quotes that matter most get buried.
  • It invents patterns that sound right but don't hold up when verified.
  • It doesn't replace being in the room with a user.

PayFit note: the research team is already testing AI-augmented approaches (see 06 Research). The rule that's emerging: AI prepares, the researcher decides.

Concept & exploration

Where it helps
  • Generating 10 variants of a flow to get past the first reflex.
  • Prototyping a happy path in minutes to test a stance before investing.
  • Thinking out loud with a model to sharpen an intuition.
Where it overpromises
  • The 10 variants look more alike than we realize. AI's conformity bias is strong.
  • It produces interfaces that look right but fail the first user test.
  • It doesn't replace the productive difficulty of finding your own idea.

Craft & production

Where it helps
  • Mobile adaptation of an existing desktop component.
  • Consistency QA across a batch of screens, DS drift, typography mistakes.
  • Structured spec writing from a mockup.
Where it overpromises
  • It applies the DS mechanically. Cases where the rule doesn't apply, it forces.
  • Output quality depends entirely on DS input quality. Ours isn't AI-ready yet (see 07 DS).
  • It makes "correct" faster, not "right".
Reality check

AI doesn't compensate for missing infrastructure. Until the Design System is machine-readable, we're patching, and any skill we build will be bound by what we can manually feed into it.

Handoff & communication

Where it helps
  • Changelogs, release notes, review notes for stakeholders.
  • Translating a design stance into an argument for a PM or engineer.
  • Presentation prep from specs.
Where it overpromises
  • It generates text that sounds professional but lacks conviction.
  • It erases the nuances that make a stance valuable.
  • It doesn't replace a face-to-face when there's actual disagreement.

Early learnings
How we work

Early learnings.

What the first designers to use AI tools in their daily work have discovered. Patterns, not prescriptions. The goal is for these to stop being rare knowledge and start being the team's common ground.

On quality, from content design

Content designers have been the first to hit AI's quality wall head-on. A few things they've noticed that the rest of the team hasn't, yet.

  • AI-generated copy passes the quick read, fails the second read. It sounds professional, flows cleanly, and uses the right-looking vocabulary. Two days later, when you re-read it with fresh eyes, the voice is generic and the choices are unjustifiable. The first instinct is always to ship it. The discipline is to re-read.
  • Brand voice is invisible to the model. Unless you give Claude a tight skill with real examples and explicit tone rules, it defaults to "SaaS corporate polite". That default is the enemy of any brand that wants to sound like itself.
  • The biggest wins are editorial, not generative. Asking Claude to write copy yields mediocre results. Asking Claude to rewrite, tighten, flag inconsistencies, or check tone against a reference yields consistently useful output. The role shift is from author to editor-with-a-very-fast-first-draft.
  • Feeding the DS manually works, but it's brittle. Exporting design system guidelines page by page into .txt files and feeding them into a custom skill is the current workaround while no proper machine-readable source exists. It produces acceptable results today. It will not scale past a few skills without proper infrastructure (see 07 DS).

On research and discovery

  • AI is a great rubber duck, a poor interviewer. Thinking out loud with Claude to sharpen a research question works. Letting Claude run a synthesis alone produces a smooth narrative that hides the uncomfortable quotes.
  • Transcripts first, summaries never. Always keep the raw transcripts. Summaries generated early in the process quietly become "the source of truth" and weak signals disappear.

On working across functions

  • PMs and engineers producing interface work is not always a threat. Often it's a shortcut that lets everyone move faster. The question is whether design reviews happen, not whether design initiates. When design is absent from the loop entirely, that's the warning sign, not the fact that someone else prototyped first.
  • Asking for AI-ready guidelines is asking for design leadership. When non-designers request "the UX guidelines to feed into Claude", it's not an attack. It's a request for the design team to codify what used to live in reviewers' heads.

How we grow this page

Contribute

This page grows from what the team learns. If you've worked with AI enough to have an opinion others don't have yet, share it. Bring it to the monthly deep-dive (see Sharing cadence), or add it directly to the Use cases library with a tag. Learnings that keep coming up get promoted to this page in the quarterly review.

Prerequisites
Getting set up

Prerequisites.

Before installing anything, make sure these three things are in place. If any of them is missing, pair with a teammate before going further.

1. A working terminal

On macOS, the default Terminal app or iTerm2 both work. You don't need to become a terminal expert, you just need to be comfortable typing a command and reading the output.

Quick check: open your terminal and run:

echo "hello"

If you see hello printed back, you're good.

2. Node.js installed

Claude Code runs on Node. Check if it's already installed:

node --version

If you get a version number (v18 or higher), skip to the next section. If you get command not found, install Node via nodejs.org, the LTS version is fine.

If this step blocks

Don't spend more than 15 minutes here alone. Ping a teammate. This is exactly what peer support is for.

3. A GitHub account tied to PayFit

You need a GitHub account linked to your PayFit email. If you don't have one yet, request access through IT.

Why: many designer use cases eventually touch repos, reading code, opening a PR with a small change, pulling the latest DS tokens. You don't need to know git deeply, but you need the account.

Install Claude Code
Getting set up

Install Claude Code.

From zero to Claude running in your terminal. If you're blocked at any step longer than 15 minutes, pair. Don't spiral alone.

Step 1, install the CLI

In your terminal, run:

npm install -g @anthropic-ai/claude-code

If you get a permission error about EACCES, don't use sudo. Instead, fix your npm permissions by following the official npm guide, or ask a teammate.

Step 2, authenticate

Run:

claude

The first time, it will open a browser to sign in with your PayFit-linked Anthropic account. If PayFit hasn't provisioned your account yet, see the License & access page.

Step 3, first run

Still in the terminal, navigate to a folder where you want to work. Could be a scratch folder, could be a repo.

cd ~/Documents/design-sandbox
claude

Claude starts an interactive session. Try:

> Create a markdown file with a short brief for a new onboarding screen

If you see a file appear in the folder, it works. You're in.

Common errors

Symptom What to try
command not found: claude The install didn't finish. Re-run the install command, check for errors in the output.
EACCES permission denied Don't use sudo. Fix npm permissions or ask a teammate.
Authentication loop in browser Your PayFit Anthropic account likely isn't provisioned yet. See License & access.
Works in terminal, not in VS Code VS Code has a different shell. Restart VS Code after install. If it persists, check VS Code's integrated terminal settings.

CLAUDE.md setup
Getting set up

CLAUDE.md, global and project.

CLAUDE.md is the file Claude reads every time it starts. It's how you teach Claude who you are, how you work, and what rules apply. Two scopes: global (all your work) and project (this repo only).

Why it matters

Without a CLAUDE.md, Claude starts every session as a generalist. With one, it arrives already knowing the DS tokens, the tone of voice, the conventions of your project. Every prompt gets shorter. Every output gets closer to right on the first try.

Global CLAUDE.md

Lives at ~/.claude/CLAUDE.md. Applies to every Claude session on your machine. Keep it short, this is about you, not about any specific project.

Template to start from:

# About me

I'm a product designer at PayFit.
I work in Figma and Notion daily. I'm not a full-stack engineer.

## How to respond
- Be concrete, skip preambles.
- If the task is visual, show me markup I can preview, not theory.
- Flag anything that touches the DS, don't assume, ask.
- If you write copy, write it in English unless I say otherwise.

## What I care about
- Unity DS compliance (Inter, semantic tokens, no primitives).
- Content clarity over cleverness.
- Small, reviewable outputs, not 500-line monoliths.

Project CLAUDE.md

Lives at the root of a project folder. Applies only when Claude runs in that folder. This is where project-specific context goes, design system references, tone of voice for this product, folder structure.

# Project: Onboarding redesign

## Context
This is the onboarding flow for new PayFit customers (admins, not employees).
Figma source: [link]
DS reference: Unity, see /docs/unity-tokens.md

## Conventions
- All copy in English first, French second.
- Mobile and desktop specs are separate files.
- Every screen needs an empty state.

## Don't
- Don't invent new components. Propose a variant of an existing Unity one.
- Don't use warning yellow for destructive actions. Danger is red.
Rule of thumb

Global = who you are. Project = what this work is. When in doubt, put it in the project file, you can always promote it to global later.

MCP & Figma
Getting set up

MCP & Figma.

MCP is the power outlet between Claude and the tools you already use. Once wired up, Claude can read your Figma file, pull DS tokens, open tasks, and respond with context instead of guesses.

What is an MCP?

MCP stands for Model Context Protocol. It's a standard way for Claude to talk to external tools, Figma, GitHub, Notion, your company's DS, through a server that exposes a specific set of actions.

You don't need to understand the protocol. You need to know: if a tool has an MCP server, Claude can use it directly, no manual copy-paste.

The Figma Console MCP

This is the one that changes the most for designers. With the Figma MCP connected, Claude can:

  • Read the structure of a Figma file you're working on.
  • Export component specs automatically.
  • Cross-check a screen against Unity DS tokens.
  • Generate a handoff doc from a frame, without you having to screenshot.

Setup

1. Enable Dev Mode in Figma

The MCP runs through Figma's Dev Mode. Make sure your PayFit Figma seat has Dev Mode enabled. If not, ask the DS team.

2. Add the MCP to Claude Code

In your terminal:

claude mcp add figma

Claude will walk you through the authentication.

3. Test

Open a Claude Code session in a project folder. Try:

> List the frames in the Figma file [paste URL]
> Check if frame "Onboarding step 2" uses only Unity semantic tokens
The Zeroheight question

A Zeroheight MCP doesn't exist today. Until it does, DS guidelines need to be exported manually and fed to Claude via custom skills. It's not elegant, but it works. See Team skills for examples.

Priority skills
Getting set up

Priority skills.

Three Claude Code skills we think the team should invest in first. These are hypotheses, not mandates. They'll be confirmed, pivoted, or expanded based on what the team self-assessment and the use cases library surface.

How we picked three

A Claude Code skill is a named, reusable capability: install it once, invoke it anywhere. Instead of re-explaining context every time, the skill carries it.

For this V1 we prioritize skills that:

  1. Automate a repetitive task most designers face weekly, not just one role.
  2. Produce outputs that can be compared to a non-AI workflow (quality, time, satisfaction).
  3. Don't require DS infrastructure we don't have yet.

1. Content Design skill

Status: already started. Built manually by the content design team. Needs consolidation and sharing.

What it does: carries PayFit's content conventions (tone of voice, inclusive language, Zeroheight content rules) and applies them when Claude produces or reviews any copy.

Who it helps: every designer who writes UI copy. Not just content designers.

Why now: content is the easiest place to spot AI quality drift (see Early learnings). A shared skill raises the floor.

2. DS QA skill

Status: hypothesis to validate.

What it does: takes a batch of screens and flags Unity DS drift. Primitive tokens where semantic ones should be, wrong typography scale, missing interactive states (especially disabled), accessibility gaps.

Who it helps: product and service designers shipping fast, who today rely on peer review catching DS slips.

Why now: QA is slow, tedious, and easy to delegate without losing judgment. High signal, low risk.

3. Behaviour Spec skill

Status: hypothesis to validate.

What it does: turns a mockup into a structured specification for engineering handoff, covering states, edge cases, empty states, error states, transitions. The stuff we always mean to document but rarely have time for.

Who it helps: anyone doing handoff. Solves the "I spent two hours writing a spec nobody reads" problem.

What a useful Behaviour Spec skill needs

Because this is the trickiest of the three, here's the target contract. A skill that meets this bar is worth building. A skill that doesn't isn't worth shipping.

Input the designer provides

  • The artifact, a Figma frame URL or an image of the mockup.
  • The scope, a one-line intention: "this is the payroll confirmation screen, admin sees it once per month."
  • Known constraints, anything non-obvious: user role, data dependencies, locked decisions.

Output the skill produces

  1. A structured list of states, default, loading, empty, error, success, partial, offline, disabled. Each state named, each state with a one-sentence description of when it appears.
  2. Interaction specs, what each interactive element does on click, hover, focus, long press, keyboard navigation. Flags elements that aren't reachable by keyboard.
  3. Edge cases and questions, a short list of things the designer hasn't decided yet, formatted as questions not assumptions. Example: "If the user has no payroll history, should we show the empty state or a pre-created mock?"
  4. DS references, the Unity components used and the tokens they rely on, so engineering can plug in the right building blocks.
  5. What's explicitly out of scope, one line naming what this spec does not cover, to prevent scope drift in engineering review.

What it must never do

  • Invent business rules, compliance logic, or data schemas.
  • Decide the questions in section 3. It lists them, the designer answers them.
  • Assume a component exists in Unity without checking. If unsure, it flags.
Quality bar

A spec is useful when an engineer can start implementation the same day without a meeting, and when the questions listed are the questions they would have asked anyway. If the spec generates follow-up questions that were already implicitly answered in the mockup, it's too shallow.

How we confirm or pivot these three

These three are proposals. The confirmation loop is simple:

  1. Two weeks of real use by at least two designers per skill.
  2. A short review in the next biweekly sharing session: what it did well, what it got wrong, was it worth the setup cost.
  3. If a proposed skill doesn't earn its keep, we pivot. If another pattern emerges from the Use cases library, we promote it.

Evaluating new tools
Experiments

Evaluating new tools.

New AI design tools appear every week. We can't test all of them, and we shouldn't. A lightweight grid to decide what's worth our time.

A tool earns integration when

  1. It solves a problem we've already identified, not the other way around.
  2. It survives two weeks of real use by at least two designers.
  3. Its outputs can be compared to the non-AI workflow on quality, time, and satisfaction.
  4. It doesn't create a dependency we can't back out of.

We don't integrate when

  • It's impressive in the demo and silent in the test.
  • It forces us to restructure our workflow around its terms, not ours.
  • It makes AI do what we decided never to delegate.

How we run an experiment

The criteria above are the bar. Here's the process that takes a tool from "someone saw it on Twitter" to "we use it or we don't", without disrupting anyone's current work.

1. Propose

Anyone on the team can propose a tool. The proposal fits in three lines, posted in the design AI channel:

  • What the tool is and what it claims to do.
  • Which problem we already have that it might solve.
  • Which existing criterion from "A tool earns integration when" it could meet.

No formal review yet. Proposals that no one signs up to test within two weeks are closed. That's a signal: if no one has the bandwidth or the need, it's not the right moment.

2. Sign up

A proposal needs at least two designers willing to run it for two weeks in their real work. Not a side project, not a dedicated sprint. Actual, in-flight design tasks.

Why two: one designer alone reports too subjectively. Two designers disagree, and the disagreement is where the signal lives.

3. Run for two weeks

During those two weeks, the experimenters keep a light log. A shared doc with three columns is enough:

  • Task: what they used the tool for.
  • Result: did it help, hurt, or make no difference, in one sentence.
  • Would recommend: a simple yes/no/it depends, with a one-line reason.

No slide decks, no benchmarks, no KPIs. The log is evidence, not performance.

4. Decide, in 15 minutes

At the next biweekly sharing session (see Sharing cadence), the experimenters present their log. The decision has three possible outcomes:

  • Integrate: the tool becomes part of the team's stack. Someone owns writing the short guide for the rest of the team. Done within a week of the decision.
  • Pass: the tool isn't worth our time right now. Logged with a one-line reason so we don't re-test the same thing in three months.
  • Extend: needs another two weeks with different use cases or more designers. Max one extension. After that, pass by default.

5. Ring-fence the risk

The process has one hard rule: no experiment goes on a critical path. If a designer is on a release-blocking task, they don't also run the experiment on that task. Experiments happen on work where a suboptimal output is a learning, not a blocker.

Why this shape

Lightweight enough that the process doesn't eat the exploration. Structured enough that we don't keep re-testing the same hype. The log and the 15-minute decision are the minimum that turns individual opinion into team memory.

Sharing cadence
Experiments

Sharing cadence.

A framework that doesn't evolve is a framework we don't use. Three rhythms keep this alive.

Every What happens
2 weeks 30 minutes, team-wide. 2 or 3 use cases from the library discussed. What worked, what broke.
1 month A deep-dive use case presented by one designer. 15 minutes plus 15 minutes of questions.
1 quarter Framework review. What evolved? What do we remove? What do we add? Self-assessment refresh.

If any of these rhythms stops happening, that's the signal the framework has become theoretical. At that point, we either revive it or admit it's not working and rebuild.
